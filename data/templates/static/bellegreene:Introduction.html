The Correspondence of Belle da Costa Greene and Bernard Berenson

Nearly 600 letters written by Belle da Costa Greene (1879–1950) to Bernard Berenson (1865–1959) comprise one of the twentieth century's most compelling groups of personal correspondence.

Written by the formidable and brilliant librarian and director of the Morgan Library to the preeminent art historian of the age, the letters open a window onto the compelling world of rare books, art, and society in New York, 1910–1950. Since the letters Berenson wrote to Greene do not survive, this correspondence is one of the only extant sources documenting their relationship.

Belle Greene and Bernard Berenson

Greene and Berenson first met in New York in January 1909. Berenson was there with his wife, Mary, and together they visited the Morgan Library to view its treasures. At that point Greene had been working for the financier and collector J. Pierpont Morgan (1837–1913) for over three years, since the summer of 1905, as his Librarian. Belle and Bernard became friendly and soon began a romantic relationship, largely long-distance and expressed through letters. The first letter is dated February 23, 1909 and their correspondence would continue for five decades, until the final letter of December 1948.

Personal Branding

Both Belle and Bernard developed monograms or personal logos that they used for stationery and other applications.

The stylized "Belle" enclosed in a circle decorated one of the many types of stationery that Greene used for her personal correspondence. The "Belle" stationery appears as late as 1930, though she used it more frequently in the 1910s.

The "BB" monogram, with its apian visual pun, was designed for Berenson by the stage designer and theater director Edward Gordon Craig probably around 1907-1908. Berenson seldom used it in his correspondence or books, preferring for the latter the ex libris indicating joint possession with his wife Mary. The playful motif of the bee recurred, however, in a charming pebble mosaic embedded in the 1910s in the new garden of Berenson's home, I Tatti.

Destroyed Letters

Two factors make these letters particularly important. First, Belle Greene destroyed her personal papers before her death in 1950, including all of Bernard Berenson's letters and her private diaries. For this reason, the personal letters she sent to friends are particularly valuable in reconstructing details of her life.

Detailed Life Writing

Second, it seems Berenson asked Greene to include as much detail as possible in her letters, and indeed many of them are quite long and read almost like journal entries. Greene did not always enjoy writing so minutely about her life, describing one of her letters to Berenson as "a small beer chronicle."

The result is a treasure trove of Belle Greene's words, expressed confidently to her friend, intellectual peer, and sometime romantic partner. These letters, in addition to the professional papers held at the Morgan Library & Museum, capture the vibrancy, wit, and confidence of Greene's voice.

"Did I tell you that for the first time in my life I am keeping a diary? It is the greatest relief to find a dear white sheet before you that you know will be silent as the grave & then to tell it unreservedly all one thinks. I write down everything I see or hear during the day, all my personal impressions & feelings and have been surprised at the number and variety of events it records for me – I shall never allow a living soul to see it & for that reason I write things I hardly dare think."

Belle Greene to Bernard Berenson, 12 September 1909

Researching the Letters

Greene's letters to Berenson have received the most attention in Heidi Ardizzone's 2007 biography of Greene, An Illuminated Life: Belle da Costa Greene's Journey from Prejudice to Privilege. But scholars before her, most notably Jean Strouse, also read these letters. Strouse would famously reveal in 1999 that Belle da Costa Greene was actually Belle Marion Greener, daughter of the activist and lawyer Richard T. Greener, the first Black graduate of Harvard College.

Belle Greene's Family

In 1874 Greener married Genevieve Ida Fleet, who descended from an elite Black family in Georgetown. Born in 1879, Belle was their second surviving child. Both Richard and Genevieve were light in complexion and had been listed as "Mulatto" or even "White" in census records. When the family moved to New York City in the 1880s rumors circulated that Genevieve was passing as white.

Passing in NYC

Around 1896 Greene's parents split up, Genevieve took the children, and they would begin passing as white. Belle and her brother Russell adopted the middle name "da Costa" and claimed Portuguese heritage while Genevieve transformed "Fleet" into the Dutch-sounding "Van Vliet." In the 1920 U.S. Census Greene's father's ancestry is listed as "Spanish," and she would claim her mother had "grand southern blood" (they pointed to origins in Virginia).

Digitizing and Presenting the Letters

Previous studies have made much of the Greene–Berenson correspondence, but the letters have remained unpublished and accessible only to scholars using the Biblioteca Berenson at I Tatti. In 2019, as part of their preparations for a major 2024 exhibition on Greene, the Morgan Library & Museum funded the digitization of the full set of letters. Miraculously, I Tatti staff were able to complete digitization of the letters before Northern Italy became the epicenter of the COVID-19 pandemic in March 2020.

Over the ensuing years various members of the Morgan's curatorial staff have worked with colleagues at I Tatti to build intellectual context around the letters and a digital platform for making them accessible to researchers. The Morgan team has contributed the bulk of the transcriptions, metadata, and ancillary website material, while librarians and digital humanists at I Tatti have facilitated access to the letters and translated the Morgan's work into a humanities data environment and publicly available website.

In one of her final letters to Berenson, written in 1945, Greene is happy to hear from her old friend, whose letter takes her back to happier times: "I cannot tell you what it meant to me – just like the dear gone days to hear from you again. In these terrible days I (occasionally!) remind myself that after all we knew a grand time and lived a grand life." The letters compiled on this website offer one of many perspectives on that "grand life," in all of its fascinating complexity and richness.

High-resolution images of Greene's letters to Berenson can also be accessed through the Bernard and Mary Berenson Papers Finding Aid.
