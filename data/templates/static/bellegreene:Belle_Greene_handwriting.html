Belle Greene's
Handwriting

During her lifetime Belle Greene primarily employed two distinct forms of handwriting, or hands, to write letters, notes, and professional documents.

One is a hand she seems to have learned in a library science summer course at Amherst College in the summer of 1900. She would later use this hand to write out catalogue cards at the Morgan Library.

Greene used this script in much of the handwritten documentation she produced while working at J. Pierpont Morgan's library, including cataloging documents (cards, lists, accession books), notes on collection objects, and professional correspondence. After 1925 she began to favor this hand even in her private correspondence.

The majority of Greene's letters to Berenson were written in her cursive handwriting, which could achieve a more rapid execution as a "running" hand, though at the expense of legibility. Greene's cursive hand, especially in her earliest letters to Berenson from 1909, can be difficult to read. She frequently refers to her writing as "scrawl" or "a scrawl," and it appears she used this word not only to refer to bad handwriting or penmanship but also hastily written or short letters.

While training transcribers for the project, Morgan Library & Museum staff developed visual "cheat sheets" of the letters, numbers, and common words used by Greene in her correspondence. Many of Greene's minuscule (i.e., lower case) letters can be tricky to read because they resemble other common letters (e, r, l, m, n, u), take variant forms (e, r, t), and/or diverge considerably in appearance from more familiar cursive forms (h, p). The full set of cheat sheets can be accessed via PDF here.

Occasionally, unanticipated material conditions affected Greene's handwriting, such as the failure of a pen, unsuitability of available writing implements, poor paper quality, and disruptive vibrations of car and train travel. Belle Greene used a variety of stationery during her four decades of correspondence with Berenson. The stationery documents a number of biographical details about Greene and the Morgan Library, as the letterhead cycles through several of her personal addresses and various permutations of the Morgan Library's graphic identity on paper.
